2025–26 Overview

A stable, no-crash competition season

In the preliminary design of my second year on the subteam, I moved from wing and aileron analysis into fuselage aerodynamics and propulsion structure study. That meant selecting the lifting-body fuselage airfoil, designing the motor mounts and arm supports, and building assembly jigs to make wing manufacturing repeatable.

The aircraft flew stably throughout the entire competition, even during heavy crosswind and rain, without a single crash.

Competition result: 3rd place, Design Report · 4th place overall

Mission requirement: design, build, and fly an autonomous hybrid fixed-wing/VTOL aircraft under 3.5 lb, capable of precisely picking up and dropping off a payload without human intervention.

Focus

What I worked on

01 Designed motor arm supports to carry 920 g of static propeller thrust plus the torsional loads it introduces into the wing.
02 Designed SolidWorks CAD models for the motor mounts and arm supports of the autonomous hybrid tricopter, with an eye toward manufacturability and ease of assembly.
03 Selected the lifting-body fuselage airfoil from 14 candidates via XFLR5 analysis, balancing aerodynamic performance against a hard internal height constraint from a tall onboard GPS unit.
04 Designed modular assembly jigs to standardize wing manufacturing and remove recurring fuselage-alignment and wing-warping defects from previous seasons.
05 Manufactured and repaired 4 pairs of composite wings, using balsa wood, carbon fiber, and heat-shrink film, across 10+ test flights.

Design Process

From airfoil selection to a no-crash flight record

01

Constrained airfoil selection

The fuselage needed a lifting-body airfoil that could house a tall onboard GPS module, requiring roughly 20% thickness-to-chord ratio and a flat bottom to keep the GPS level, while still holding a stable CL/CD profile at the aircraft's operating Reynolds number. Evaluated 14 candidate airfoils in XFLR5 and decided on an adapted GOE561 with a flattened bottom surface to fit the internal-volume and manufacturing constraints, rather than chasing a theoretical CL/CD optimum that couldn't physically house all the electronics.

02

Motor mount and arm design

Designed the motor mount and arm to transfer the full static thrust load (measured at 920 g) from the motor into the arm, and from the arm into the wing structure, accounting for the torsional load the thrust introduces. Sized the components using the team's established structural margins for this type of load, rather than running a dedicated FEA study.

03

Assembly jigs for manufacturing repeatability

Previous seasons had persistent fuselage alignment and wing warping issues traced back to freehand assembly. Designed modular jigs that fixed the wing in position, constraining all six degrees of freedom and eliminating dimensional deviation between built wings and turning wing manufacturing into a repeatable process instead of a source of build-to-build variation.

04

Composite build and flight-test cycle

Manufactured 4 pairs of composite wings from balsa wood, carbon fiber, and heat-shrink film, repairing and iterating across 10+ test flights ahead of competition.

05

Competition — zero-crash flight record

At competition, the aircraft completed its flight circuit manually in heavy crosswind and rain without a single crash, validating the season's airfoil, structural, and manufacturing decisions under real conditions.

Challenges

Where the hard problems were

A GPS module that dictated the fuselage airfoil

The onboard GPS required roughly 20% internal thickness and a flat mounting surface, ruling out most conventional low-drag airfoils. Rather than search for a theoretical optimum, evaluated 14 candidates in XFLR5 against the internal volume constraint and adapted GOE561 with a flattened bottom, prioritizing a manufacturable, flyable shape over a marginal aerodynamic gain the electronics couldn't fit inside.

Sizing for motor structural load

With a 920 g static thrust load to carry and no resources for a full numerical torsional stress analysis, the motor arm and mount had to be sized from the team's established structural margins on this class of load (with experimental validation) rather than a dedicated simulation. It was a trade-off between analysis time and build time — one that held up through the season's test flights and the competition itself.

Manufacturing repeatability

Wing warping and fuselage interface misalignment were recurring issues from prior seasons of freehand assembly. Modular jigs fixed each wing during manufacturing, removing dimensional deviation between builds almost entirely.

Flying through weather

The competition flight encountered intermittent heavy crosswinds and rain, conditions under which the airframe had not been extensively tested before. Despite this, the season's structural modifications maintained their integrity throughout the event, with no crashes, a marked improvement over the previous year's instability.

2024–25 Overview

Building the platform, chasing stability

In my first year on the subteam, I designed the CAD for the aircraft's wings and ailerons and worked on composite manufacturing, while the team spent much of the season fighting flight instability. The root cause was an undersized horizontal stabilizer that wasn't identified until late in the year, after most of the season's testing and repairs were already behind us.

Competition result: 1st place, Design Report · 5th place overall

Mission requirement: design, build, and fly an autonomous hybrid fixed-wing/VTOL aircraft under 3.5 lb, capable of precisely picking up and dropping off a payload without human intervention.

Focus

What I worked on

01 Designed SolidWorks CAD models for the wings and ailerons of the autonomous hybrid tricopter, considering manufacturability and ease of assembly.
02 Manufactured and repaired 2 pairs of composite wings, using balsa wood, carbon fiber, and heat-shrink film, across 7 test flights.
03 Researched frise ailerons and other methods to counteract adverse yaw instability observed in flight testing.

Design Process

A season spent diagnosing instability

01

Wing and aileron CAD

Designed the wing and aileron geometry in SolidWorks, working within the competition's 3.5 lb weight ceiling while keeping the structure manufacturable.

02

Composite construction

Built 2 pairs of wings from balsa wood, carbon fiber, and heat-shrink film, repairing wing structure between test flights as damage occurred.

03

Diagnosing adverse yaw

Flight testing showed adverse yaw instability. Researched frise ailerons as a mechanical fix, alongside other adverse-yaw mitigation approaches used on similar airframes.

04

Choosing a software fix over a mechanical one

We ultimately didn't implement frise ailerons. At this aircraft's scale, small manufacturing inaccuracies have an outsized effect on airflow attachment and aerodynamic performance, and without the tooling to validate the geometry beforehand, committing material and flight-test time to an unproven design felt too risky. Adverse yaw was instead minimized through software — differential aileron deflection — a lower-risk fix given those validation constraints.

05

Finding the real cause, late

Late in the year, the actual root cause of the repeated hard landings and crashes was identified as an undersized horizontal stabilizer. Although most of the season's flight tests had already been completed, the stabilizer was redesigned in time for a successful competition flight.

Challenges

Where the hard problems were

Adverse yaw at small scale

Frise ailerons were a well-estabilished mechanical solution to adverse yaw. However, at this aircraft's scale, small manufacturing inaccuracies could significantly alter the local airflow and promote premature flow separation, effects the team couldn't confidently evaluate beforehand. Differential aileron deflection was therefore selected as a control-system solution instead of committing build time to an unvalidated mechanical change.

Uncovering the source of instability

Repeated hard landings and crashes triggered a season-long root-cause investigation with several hypotheses being explored. However, the actual root cause, an undersized horizontal stabilizer, wasn't confirmed until late in the season, a lesson that directly shaped the following year's structural priorities.

Repairing composite wings mid-season

Built and repaired 2 pairs of wings across 7 test flights, with each flight test run multiple times, keeping the aircraft flight-ready under repeated hard-landing damage.
